About These Dishes

Fried Rice (炒饭 chao fan)

A classic stir-fried rice dish with vegetables, egg, soy sauce, and sometimes meat or seafood. Savory, aromatic, and popular across China.

Chow Mein (炒面 chao mian)

Stir-fried noodles cooked with vegetables, soy sauce, and sometimes meat or tofu. Can be soft or crispy depending on the region.

Sweet and Sour Chicken (糖醋鸡 tang cu ji)

Crispy chicken pieces coated in a sweet and sour sauce made from vinegar, sugar, and fruit or tomato. Bright, sticky, and popular worldwide.

Dongpo Pork (东坡肉 dong po rou)

Braised pork belly cooked slowly in soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, and sugar. Known for its melt-in-your-mouth texture and caramelized flavor.

Peking Duck (北京烤鸭 Beijing kao ya)

Beijing specialty with crispy skin and tender meat, served with pancakes, scallions, cucumber, and sweet bean sauce.

Dumplings / Jiaozi (饺子 jiao zi)

Thin dough wrappers filled with meat and vegetables, boiled, steamed, or pan-fried. Often eaten during Chinese New Year for luck.

Mapo Tofu (麻婆豆腐 ma po dou fu)

Spicy Sichuan tofu in chili and peppercorn sauce with minced pork or beef. Famous for its numbing and spicy "mala" flavor.

Spring Rolls (春卷 chun juan)

Thin rolls filled with vegetables or meat, then deep fried until crispy. Traditional festival food symbolizing wealth and spring.

Tomato and Egg Stir-Fry (番茄炒蛋 fan qie chao dan)

Simple home-style dish of scrambled eggs and tomatoes. Comfort food often eaten with rice and a favorite in many families.

Wonton Soup (馄饨汤 hun tun tang)

Light broth soup with delicate wontons filled with pork or shrimp. Comforting and found everywhere from street stalls to restaurants.

Xiao Long Bao (小笼包 xiao long bao)

Soup dumplings from Shanghai filled with pork and hot broth that bursts when you bite into them. Known for their delicate structure.
